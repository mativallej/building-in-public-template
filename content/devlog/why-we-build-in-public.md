We are two people building Acme Tasks, a small task manager for small teams. We started a private beta in November 2025, and we decided from the start to build it in public. This post is a plain explanation of why, so that when we post a good week or a bad one, you know what we were trying to do.

## Accountability we can't wriggle out of

A private number is easy to reason around. You tell yourself the churn was seasonal, the flat signups were a fluke, the roadmap slipped because priorities changed. A public number is harder to lie to. Once we write "20 paying teams" on a page that other people read, we can't quietly round it up in our own heads next month.

Building in public forces every decision to be defensible. Before we ship something, we ask whether we'd be comfortable explaining it in a devlog. If the honest explanation is "we did it because it was easy" or "we hoped nobody would notice," that usually means we shouldn't do it. That filter alone has changed a few calls.

## Marketing by being honest

We are not good at hype, and we don't want to fake it. What we can do is show the actual work: the beta, the paid-onboarding experiment we ran in early 2026 that flopped, and the free tier we shipped in April 2026. Telling that story straight seems to attract the right kind of user, people who want a small tool run by people they can reach, not a growth machine.

> We would rather have 300 users who understand the tradeoffs than 3,000 who feel misled the first time something breaks.

Here is roughly how we split what goes out and what stays in:

| What we publish | What we keep private |
| --- | --- |
| Signups, active users, MRR | Individual customer identities |
| Product decisions and reasons | Unannounced pricing changes |
| Experiments that failed | Anything a user shared in confidence |
| Rough monthly costs | Our own salaries and personal finances |

The line is simple. We publish numbers about the business. We do not publish anything that belongs to a specific person who trusted us with it.

A few of the numbers we are living with right now:

|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| 1,234 | tasks created |
| 312 | weekly active users |
| $1,000 | MRR |

None of these are big. That is part of the point. If we only talked about the good weeks, the story would be dishonest, and dishonest is the one thing building in public is supposed to prevent.

## What this costs us

Being open has real downsides, and we want to name them:

- Competitors can read our numbers as easily as our users can.
- A bad month is public, so a bad month stings twice.
- We spend a few hours a week writing instead of building.

We think the trade is worth it. The writing forces us to think clearly, the numbers keep us honest, and the people who follow along tend to give us better feedback than any survey ever has. That is the whole deal: we show the work, you get to judge it, and we get a reason to keep the work honest.

We build in public because it makes us better at building. Everything else is a side effect.
